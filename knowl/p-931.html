We may have noticed a pattern with derivatives. But to illustrate it explicitly:

  • The derivative of \(f(x)=x^0\text{,}\) it's the derivative of the constant function \(f(x)=1\text{,}\) and since its a horizontal line, it never has any slope and \(f'(x)=0\text{.}\)

  • The derivative of \(f(x)=x^1\text{,}\) it's the derivative of the line \(f(x)=x\) which always has slope 1, so \(f'(x)=1\text{.}\)

  • The derivative of \(f(x)=x^2\) is:

    \begin{align*} \frac{d}{dx}[x^2]\amp = \limit{h}{0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} \frac{(x+h)^2-x^2}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} \frac{x^2+2xh+h^2-x^2}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} \frac{(2x+h)h}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} 2x+h=2x\text{.} \end{align*}

  • The derivative of \(f(x)=x^3\) is:

    \begin{align*} \frac{d}{dx}[x^3]\amp = \limit{h}{0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} \frac{(x+h)^3-x^3}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} \frac{x^3+3x^2h+3xh^2+h^3-x^3}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} \frac{(3x^2+3xh+h^2)h}{h}\\ \amp = \limit{h}{0} 3x^2+3xh+h^2=3x^2\text{.} \end{align*}
