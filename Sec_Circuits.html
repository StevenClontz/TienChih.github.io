Section 4.6 Circuits

Consider

\begin{equation*} \begin{circuitikz} \draw (0,0) to[ospst=$p$] (2,0); \end{circuitikz} \end{equation*}

This is the diagram of the “circuit” \(p\) Imagine \(T\) as been the bridge open and \(F\) as the bridge closed. Can you cross over, well, yes as long as the bridge is open, i.e. as long as \(p\) is true.

Now how about this:

Consider

\begin{equation*} \begin{circuitikz} \draw (0,0) to[ospst=$p$] (2,0); \draw (2,0) to[ospst=$q$] (4,0); \end{circuitikz} \end{equation*}

Can you get to the other side? Well, as long as both bridges are open, i.e. \(p\wedge q\text{.}\)

How about this?

\begin{equation*} \begin{circuitikz} \draw (0,0) to (2,0); \draw (2,0) to (2,1); \draw (2,0) to (2,-1); \draw (2,1) to[ospst=$p$] (4,1); \draw (2,-1) to[ospst=$q$] (4,-1); \draw(4,1) to (4,0); \draw(4,-1) to (4,0); \draw(4,0) to (6,0); \end{circuitikz} \end{equation*}

Can you get from one side to the other? Yes, as long as either bridge is open we can, so this is \(p\vee q\text{.}\)

Using these circuit diagrams, we can model any logical statement. For example \(p\wedge ((q\wedge r) \vee (\sim q \wedge \sim r))\) would be:

\begin{equation*} \begin{circuitikz} \draw (0,0) to[ospst=$p$] (2,0); \draw (2,0) to (2,1); \draw (2,0) to (2,-1); \draw (2,1) to[ospst=$q$] (3,1); \draw (3,1) to[ospst=$r$] (4,1); \draw (2,-1) to[ospst=$\sim q$] (3,-1); \draw (3,-1) to[ospst=$\sim r$] (4,-1); \draw(4,1) to (4,0); \draw(4,-1) to (4,0); \draw(4,0) to (5,0); \end{circuitikz} \end{equation*}

And

\begin{equation*} \begin{circuitikz} \draw (0,0) to[ospst=$p$] (2,0); \draw (2,0) to (2,1); \draw (2,0) to (2,-1); \draw (2,1) to[ospst=$q$] (3,1); \draw (3,1) to[ospst=$r$] (4,1); \draw (2,-1) to (3,-1); \draw (3,-1) to (3,-.5); \draw (3,-1) to (3,-1.5); \draw (3,-.5) to[ospst=$\sim s$] (4,-.5); \draw (3,-1.5) to[ospst=$\sim t$] (4,-1.5); \draw(4,1) to (4,0); \draw(4,-1.5) to (4,0); \draw(4,0) to (5,0); \end{circuitikz} \end{equation*}

would be \(p \wedge ((q\wedge r)\vee (\sim s\vee \sim t))\text{.}\)

Here, it again helps to be able to break everything down into ands/ors. We don't have something for implications, but of we wanted to do the circuit for \(\sim((p\vee q)\to r)\text{,}\) we could note:

\begin{align*} \sim((p\vee q)\to r)\amp =\amp \sim(\sim(p\vee q)\vee r)\\ \amp =\amp (p\vee q)\wedge \sim r\text{.} \end{align*}

So it's circuit would be:

\begin{equation*} \begin{circuitikz} \draw (0,0) to (2,0); \draw (2,0) to (2,1); \draw (2,0) to (2,-1); \draw (2,1) to[ospst=$p$] (4,1); \draw (2,-1) to[ospst=$q$] (4,-1); \draw(4,1) to (4,0); \draw(4,-1) to (4,0); \draw(4,0) to[ospst=$\sim r$] (6,0); \end{circuitikz} \end{equation*}
