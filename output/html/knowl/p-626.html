So this explains why \(e\) is such a significant number in mathematics, it is the unique number so that the exponential \(e^x\) is it's own derivative \url{https://www.desmos.com/calculator/xelrg6t0ez}. Every other \(a^x\) differs from it's derivative slightly: \url{https://www.desmos.com/calculator/aqa6zw4vyz}, you can see that when \(a\) is less than \(e\) (about 2.714), it's derivative falls under the original function, and when \(a>e\text{,}\) it's derivative falls above the original.
