Section 6.3 Polynomials

Definition 6.3.1.

A polynomial \(p(x)\) is a function that can be written in the form

\begin{equation*} p(x)=a_nx^n+a_{n-1}x^{n-1}+\cdots+a_1x^1+a_0(x^0)\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(n\geq 0\) is a whole number, and \(a_n\neq 0\) unless \(p(x)=0\text{.}\)

  • The \(a_i\) are the coefficients of \(p(x)\text{.}\)

  • \(a_n\) is the leading coefficient of \(p(x)\text{.}\)

  • \(n\) is the degree of \(p(x)\text{.}\)

  • \(a_nx^n\) is the leading term of \(p(x)\text{.}\)

We've already seen some examples of these.

  • A constant linear function \(y=c\) is a polynomial of degree 0, since \(y=cx^0\text{.}\)
  • A constant linear function of the form \(y=mx+b\) with non-zero slope is a polynomial of degree 1, since \(y=mx^1+bx^0\text{.}\)
  • A constant quadratic function \(y=ax^2+bx+c\) is a polynomial of degree 2, since \(y=ax^2+bx^1+cx^0\text{.}\)

Subsection 6.3.1 Long Term Behavior

When we consider a polynomial \(p(x)=a_nx^n+a_{n-1}x^{n-1}+\cdots+a_1x^1+a_0\text{,}\) we notice that no matter how big the coefficients \(a_{n-1}\) through \(a_0\) are, they will all eventually be made insignificant by \(x^n\) for some sufficiently large values of \(x\text{.}\) Thus we have the following fact:

Remark 6.3.2.

The long term behavior of \(p(x)\) is determined completely by it's leading term \(a_nx^n\text{.}\)

To see this, consider the plots of \(y=x^3\) and \(y=1000x^2\text{.}\) One thousand is a huge coefficient for sure! But will that matter so much when \(x\) is in the thousands, the millions, the trillions? Eventually \(x\cdot x^2\) will be much greater than \(1000\cdot x^2\) when \(x\) is big enough. So if we plotted \(y=x^3-1000x^2\) next to \(y=x^3\) We see that they have the same general shape.

So we have 2 things to consider, whether or not \(a_n\) is positive or negative, or whether or not \(n\) is even or odd.

If \(a_n>0\text{,}\) then there are 2 possibilities:

  • If \(n\) is even, (like \(x^2, x^4, x^6)\text{,}\) then \(a_nx^n\) cannot be negative, so \(p(x)\) will tend to positive infinity as \(x\) goes to either positive or negative infinity.

  • If \(n\) is odd, (like \(x, x^3, x^5)\text{,}\) then \(a_nx^n\) is positive as long as \(x\) is positive, so \(p(x)\) will tend to positive infinity as \(x\) goes to either positive infinity, and \(a_nx^n\) will go to negative infinity as x goes to negative infinity.

Then, if \(a_n\lt 0\text{,}\) everything is flipped upside down. We can summarize as follows:

  • If \(a_n>0, n\) is even, \(p(x)\to \infty \) as \(x\to \pm \infty\text{.}\)
  • If \(a_n\lt 0, n\) is even, \(p(x)\to -\infty \) as \(x\to \pm \infty\text{.}\)
  • If \(a_n>0, n\) is odd, \(p(x)\to \infty \) as \(x\to \infty\text{,}\) and \(p(x)\to -\infty \) as \(x\to -\infty\text{.}\)
  • If \(a_n\lt 0, n\) is odd, \(p(x)\to -\infty \) as \(x\to \infty\text{,}\) and \(p(x)\to \infty \) as \(x\to -\infty\text{.}\)

Subsection 6.3.2 Short Term Behavior

The first thing one can always do when analyzing the short term behavior of \(p(x)\) is find the \(y\)-intercept. The \(y\) intercept is just the point on the curve of the function when \(x=0\text{,}\) so this is \(p(0)=a_0\text{.}\)

By the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra, we can write \(p(x)=a_nx^n+\cdots +a_0\) as:

\begin{equation*} p(x)=a_n(x-r_1)^{m_1}(x-r_2)^{m_2}\cdots (x-r_k)^{m_k}\cdot q_1(x)q_2(x)\cdots q_\ell(x) \end{equation*}

where the \(q_i(x)\) are irreducible quadratics. We call \(r_1, \ldots, r_k\) the roots of \(r(x)\text{,}\) where root \(r_i\) has multiplicty \(m_i\text{.}\) What this means is if we plug \(r_i\) into \(p(x)\text{,}\) we end up with 0, since that linear factor ends up being 0.

The behavior of \(p(x)\) around these roots is determined by the multiplicity of that root. We know that \(p(x)\) will be 0 at those roots, so the question is whether or not the polynomial crosses the \(x\) axis at a given roots, or bounces off the axis:

We notice that \(p(x)\) only changes sign when it's factors \((x-r_i)^{m_i}\) changes sign. But \((x-r_i)^{m_i}\) only changes sign if \(m_i\) is odd. So we have the following fact:

Remark 6.3.3.

The polynomial \(p(x)\) “crosses” the \(x\)-axis at \(r_i\) if \(m_i\) is odd and “bounces” if \(m_i\) is even.

Example 6.3.4.

Let

\begin{equation*} p(x)=x^3-3x^2=x^2(x-3)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Since it is a odd degree polynomial with positive leading coefficient, it goes up to \(\infty\) as \(x\) goes to \(\infty\) and down to \(-\infty\) as \(x\) goes to \(-\infty\text{.}\) We also see that \(p(x)=0\) whenever \(x=0\) or \(x=3\text{.}\) The multiplicity of 0 is 2, and the multiplicity of 3 is 1, which are even and odd respectively, so \(p(x)\) “bounces” at \(x=0\) and crosses at \(x=1\text{.}\)

Example 6.3.5.

Let

\begin{equation*} p(x)=x^4-2x^2+1=(x-1)^2(x+1)^2\text{.} \end{equation*}

Since it is a even degree polynomial with positive leading coefficient, it goes up to \(\infty\) as \(x\) goes to both positive and negative \(\infty\text{.}\) We also see that \(p(x)=0\) whenever \(x=-1\) or \(x=1\text{.}\) The multiplicity of both of these roots is 2, so \(p(x)\) “bounces” at both \(x=1\) and \(x=-1\text{.}\)
